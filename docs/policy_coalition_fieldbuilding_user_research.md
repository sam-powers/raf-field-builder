# Policy Coalition Field-Building: User Research Brief

**Last updated:** April 2026 | **Version:** 2.0 — revised for source accuracy, updated AI adoption figures, added Finding 8 on feedback collection dynamics

## Question

What do we know about how policy advocacy organizations and coalition members experience field-building and coalition coordination? Specifically:
1. How do orgs currently track where they align/differ with peer orgs on policy positions?
2. What are the pain points around reading and synthesizing position papers across a coalition?
3. How do coalitions currently broker introductions, manage relationship mapping, and prepare members for cross-org meetings?
4. What are the friction points when constituent orgs have very different tech capacity and political ideology?
5. How do coalitions collect member feedback, and what fails in that process?

## Findings

### Finding 1: No standardized system exists for tracking cross-org policy position alignment — most coalitions operate on informal knowledge and memory

Across coalition practitioner guides, academic reviews, and field-building literature, there is a consistent absence of any shared infrastructure for tracking where member organizations stand on specific policy positions. The Advocacy Coalition Framework (ACF) academic literature — the dominant theoretical lens for studying these coalitions — acknowledges that "the nature and scope of joint activities between advocacy coalition members have been less articulated" (Frontiers in Political Science, 2024). In practice, the activist handbook's coalition challenges section documents a specific symptom: when network leaders "don't know who the members are; where they stand on the issue; what they are doing to further the network's purpose; or how to contact them," the coalition effectively becomes unnavigable. Coalition directors' job descriptions (2024) reflect this gap — responsibilities include "tracking and assessing the coalition's impact" and "developing annual priorities and work plans," but no specific position-mapping tools are mentioned. The de facto solution appears to be the coalition coordinator maintaining this knowledge in their head or in unstructured spreadsheets.

**Key quote (Activist Handbook):** "Members don't know who the members are; where they stand on the issue; what they are doing to further the network's purpose; or how to contact them."

### Finding 2: Position synthesis across a multi-org coalition is staff-intensive, underfunded, and cognitively expensive — and almost no tooling exists for it

Quorum's 2024 State of Government Affairs Survey (N=225, mixed-sector including corporations, trade associations, and nonprofits) found **47% of respondents cited understaffing as their top challenge**, ahead of getting lawmakers' attention (40%) and lack of funding (34%). Note: This is a mixed-sector, vendor-convenience sample — it should be read as directional rather than definitive.

The TCC Group's Gates Foundation report (2021), based on 24 interviews with coalition stakeholders, identifies "strong communications capacity" as critically underdeveloped across coalitions. The TCC Group report makes similar points about communications capacity gaps: coalitions suffer from underdeveloped communications infrastructure, manual and inefficient follow-up workflows, and inability to track and report on coalition-wide impact. The synthesis burden falls almost entirely on coalition staff who must read, digest, and mentally map each member org's outputs — with no shared tooling to surface alignment or flag contradictions across documents. Notably, Quorum launched an "AI Analysis" (Copilot) feature specifically to address policy document overload, but this is scoped to legislative monitoring rather than cross-org position mapping.

**Key quote (TCC Group / Gates Foundation 2021):** Coalitions need "capacity for shared communications, tools, and connections; for strategizing across multiple organizations; for bridging differences to catalyze bigger tents of aligned interest" — and these remain chronically underfunded.

### Finding 3: Coalition relationship brokering depends almost entirely on individual coordinators — there is no shared infrastructure for warm intros or meeting preparation

The academic literature on advocacy coalitions identifies "internal coalition brokers" as foundational: "initial contact can be established by internal coalition brokers, who introduce actors to each other and thereby create the seed beds for coordination and coalition building" (Policy Studies Journal, ACF review). But this brokering function is entirely person-dependent — when that staff member leaves, institutional relationship knowledge disappears. The Community Tool Box notes coordinators must "follow up on the groups or individuals who are working on tasks assigned," with no mention of software or shared systems to manage this. Coalition director job descriptions describe responsibilities like "convene, organize, and facilitate coalition and other stakeholder/partner meetings" and "proactively identify and address the needs of coalition partners" — implying pre-meeting preparation is a large, manual, personalized task. A capable collaborative broker who "actively identifies barriers to collaborations, strategically communicates about opportunities, and brings stakeholders together" is described in the partnership literature as a key success factor — but this is a human role, not a system.

**Key insight (field-building research):** Diverse teams with a cultural broker significantly outperform those without one — but the function remains entirely manual, with no digital support layer for preparing members before cross-org meetings.

### Finding 4: Organizations protecting turf and data is a structural barrier to cross-coalition relationship mapping

The Community Tool Box's practitioner guides explicitly name organizational turf protection as one of the top coalition coordination blockers: "Organizations are often very sensitive about sharing their work, their target populations, and especially their funding." This creates resistance to the very relationship mapping and position-sharing that would make coalitions effective. The Activist Handbook adds a data security dimension: "Loose data, privacy and security practices in a coalition can lead to real harm for coalition partners and their members," which creates legitimate hesitation around sharing member-level relationship data in a centralized system. The TCC Group report flags a further structural barrier: "many funders and coalitions have made missteps that have led to harm when trying to bring on partners from excluded groups" — suggesting that even well-intentioned brokering can damage trust when not handled carefully.

### Finding 5: Tech capacity gaps between coalition members produce a two-tier coordination dynamic — large orgs carry disproportionate load, small orgs experience exclusion

The PMC health equity coalition study (LATIN-19, BCAC, 2022) surfaced a concrete example: BlackDoctor.org's technology capabilities became "essential for BCAC's reach, demonstrating uneven digital capacity across member organizations." This created a dependency rather than an equitable contribution structure. The TCC Group report documents the same pattern at scale: coalition members with "sophisticated advocacy work and important reputations need to be supported both to play substantive roles and remain incentivized, while other members make more targeted or sporadic contributions." Technical incompatibility across CRM systems, communication platforms, and data structures makes it "challenging to execute truly coordinated campaigns across partners."

On AI adoption specifically: A 2024 Quorum survey of government affairs professionals found **36% reported using AI** — with nonprofits lagging behind agencies and consultants. By 2025–2026, broader adoption surveys (TechSoup 2025 AI Benchmark Report; NonProfit PRO 2026) show AI adoption has risen sharply, though adoption definitions vary widely. The Quorum finding is best understood as a floor estimate from a self-selected vendor survey, now likely outdated. Regardless of the current figure, deep heterogeneity in tech adoption is documented across coalition member types. Small and volunteer-run member orgs often cannot keep pace with email threads, shared documents, or coalition management tools that larger orgs take for granted.

**Key pattern:** Technical incompatibility creates two-tier participation, where better-resourced orgs shape the coalition's direction and less-resourced orgs participate passively or not at all.

### Finding 6: Ideological heterogeneity creates both coalition breadth and internal fracture — and belief homophily governs who coordinates with whom

The research on coalition formation is consistent: "belief homophily plays a more important role in driving coordination in conflictual settings" (Satoh 2023, Policy Studies Journal). Cross-ideological coalitions do form around shared pragmatic interests (particularly around new issue areas like AI policy where traditional partisan alignments break down), but they face "variations in ideology, priorities, and long-term goals [that] create friction and pressure throughout the partnership." The Activist Handbook surfaces a specific manifestation: "misalignment around why it's important to center race, what it means to center race, and how to do it…can lead to painful divides." The ACF academic literature shows that "when actors have few disagreements, they form tight coalitions; at medium agreement levels, actors form an umbrella coalition wherein cooperation is broad and loose; at low agreement levels, few coalitions form and a state of disunity is expected." The practical implication: the wider the coalition's ideological span, the more the coalition staff must invest in surfacing shared interests — but no tooling exists to help staff identify these overlap zones systematically.

### Finding 7: Coalition coordination overhead falls heaviest on staff with the least support — burnout and staff turnover are an endemic risk

Across the 2024 Independent Sector nonprofit leadership interviews and Quorum's 2024 survey, nonprofit advocacy professionals described "a real feeling of having to be 'on' all the time, including knowing the pulse of the community and being available for emails and phone calls throughout the day." The TCC Group report identifies a structural leadership problem: "Effective staffing requires both good leadership and management, and coalitions have often found it challenging to find one person to play both roles." This creates a bifurcation where either coordination suffers (if the manager-heavy person is hired) or direction suffers (if the leader-heavy person is hired). The health equity coalition study documented "overextension of nurses and other health care workers at community, academic, and health system levels, leading to exhaustion from continuous volunteer effort" — a pattern generalizable to any coalition with mixed paid/volunteer staff.

### Finding 8: Coalition feedback loops are broken by design — member input is informal, episodic, and structurally skewed toward the loudest voices

Across practitioner guides, academic studies, and field research, the feedback collection function — how coalitions learn what member organizations actually think about draft policy positions, meetings, and coalition direction — is marked by consistent failures.

**Evidence 1: Feedback collection defaults to informality.**
The Community Tool Box describes member feedback as: "A coalition can decide upon and carry out less structured ways of collecting the information it needs... informal feedback questions include: 'Any comments on this meeting?'" This reveals how thin the feedback infrastructure typically is: end-of-meeting verbal check-ins as the de facto system. There is no documented standard for how coalitions systematically collect member views *before* finalizing a position.

**Evidence 2: Member input on draft positions is structurally broken.**
When coalitions formalize position-taking, the process is deliberately slow — the Coalition of Communities of Color has a two-month endorsement process requiring bimonthly committee meetings and full membership votes. The AFJ Coalition Checklist notes that "more formalized membership procedures may become an issue when coalitions wish to make public statements or endorse policy measures." In fast-moving policy environments, this means coalitions skip formal consultation and rely on informal coordinator judgment about what "the members" think — a judgment that reflects whoever the coordinator has most recently spoken to. The TCC Group / Gates Foundation report names the resulting tension: there is "inherent tension around the delineation of 'member work' and 'staff work'" and coalitions with greater staff work "may lack sufficient buy-in from members."

**Evidence 3: Survey fatigue is acute and structurally compounded for advocacy orgs.**
Research cited by Plinth (2024) found completion rates drop from 82% for surveys under five minutes to 31% for surveys over fifteen minutes. In advocacy coalition contexts, the fatigue is compounded because a single staff member at a member organization may simultaneously face survey requests from the coalition, individual funders, external evaluators, and their own org's monitoring systems — organizations with three or more funders average 4.2 separate data collection instruments per program. The CQ Roll Call nonprofit associations survey (2015, N=330 advocacy professionals) found that **36% of nonprofit associations identify engaging members as their single greatest organizational challenge**, and **75% said it is difficult or very difficult to involve members in advocacy campaigns.**

**Evidence 4: A structural vocal minority problem.**
As coalition membership increases, "coordination becomes more costly, free riding is harder to prevent, and group size affects the ability to coordinate" — resulting in "disconnected coalitions wherein actors share beliefs but do not coordinate their actions" (Frontiers in Political Science, 2024). Coalition staff hear from the same handful of active member contacts repeatedly while the majority of member organizations remain silent. Mechanisms that exist (meeting evaluations, post-meeting check-ins) only capture views of those already present — excluding those who stopped attending.

**Evidence 5: Member dissatisfaction signals are invisible until exit.**
Hirschman's Exit, Voice, and Loyalty framework applies directly: in coalition contexts where there is no formal channel for voice (no anonymous feedback mechanism, no structured dissent process), members default to loyalty (passive attendance) or quiet exit. The Community Tool Box documents: "many people leave coalitions after one or two meetings because they feel they have nothing special to offer" — with no mechanism for the coalition to know why. Thompson (2025, Policy Studies Journal) identifies that coalitions fracture, splinter, and dissolve, but "the precursor signals to breakdown are not visible in the data because coalitions lack feedback systems that would surface them early."

**Evidence 6: Power dynamics suppress candid feedback.**
Research cited by Plinth (2024, British Journal of Social Work) found **38% of nonprofit beneficiaries report feeling pressured to give positive feedback**. This dynamic generalizes to coalition member organizations: when the coalition coordinator is also the relationship broker, funder connector, and warm intro facilitator, member org staff have strong incentives to give positive or neutral feedback rather than honest critical input. The structural neutrality required of backbone organizations — which makes them effective trust-holders — simultaneously makes them poor collectors of dissenting member views.

**Evidence 7: Feedback loops rarely close.**
The Fund for Shared Insight research documents a consistent failure in nonprofit feedback practices: organizations collect input but don't "close the loop" by reporting back what changed as a result. This creates rational skepticism: if past feedback has disappeared into the coordinator's inbox without acknowledgment, there is little incentive to invest in future feedback.

**Key Quotes:**
- "Many people leave coalitions after one or two meetings because they feel they have nothing special to offer." — Community Tool Box, Coalition Building II
- "75% of nonprofit associations said it is difficult or very difficult to involve members and supporters in their advocacy campaigns." — CQ Roll Call (N=330)
- "Important groups were not always represented in crucial decisions, which generated serious tensions and resentments among the members." — PMC coalition study, Summit County

**Non-obvious finding:** "Pre-survey fatigue" — members close the survey before reading a single question because the length is visible upfront or they've been burned before. In coalition contexts, sending a formal survey may itself signal that the coalition doesn't understand member bandwidth — which is trust-eroding.

## Patterns & Pain Points

### Pattern 1: The "Coalition Coordinator as Human CRM" problem
The coalition coordinator's personal knowledge and relationships are the de facto system of record for position tracking, member alignment mapping, and relationship brokering. This creates catastrophic institutional memory loss when staff turnover occurs. No digital system adequately replaces this function today.

### Pattern 2: Document and position synthesis is fully manual
Position papers, policy briefs, and member org statements pile up in shared drives or email threads. No workflow tool helps staff surface where organizations agree, where they diverge, or what the current state of the coalition's collective position is. The synthesis task is left to individual staff cognition.

### Pattern 3: Meeting preparation is bespoke and time-consuming
Preparing coalition members for cross-org meetings — knowing who they're meeting, what those orgs have said publicly, where the interests align — is an ad hoc process with no standardized support. Coalition directors describe it as a significant part of their job but lack any tooling for it.

### Pattern 4: Tech heterogeneity creates participation tiers, not just inefficiency
The gap between large, tech-forward orgs and small, low-capacity orgs isn't merely a coordination inefficiency — it structurally excludes some members from full participation and places disproportionate coordination burden on better-resourced orgs. This can warp power dynamics and reduce the diversity of perspectives that makes coalitions politically powerful.

### Pattern 5: Ideological friction is managed through avoidance, not navigation tools
Coalitions with diverse political membership manage ideological tension by "moving at the speed of trust" (TCC Group) — a slow, relationship-intensive, resource-heavy process. There's no tooling to help map where the overlapping interests actually are, so coalition staff rely on experienced judgment and extensive relationship cultivation.

### Pattern 6: Feedback infrastructure is absent — silence is the default signal
Coalition member feedback is collected informally, episodically, and only from those already engaged. There is no mechanism to surface pre-exit dissatisfaction, no structured pre-decision consultation process, and no feedback loop closure that would make members trust that their input matters. The vocal minority becomes the de facto signal. The silent majority exits without explanation.

### Pain Points Summary

| Pain Point | Severity | Who Experiences It |

## Conclusion

Policy advocacy coalitions face a consistent, documented set of coordination failures rooted in the absence of shared digital infrastructure for four core field-building activities: (1) position tracking, (2) document synthesis, (3) relationship management and meeting preparation, and (4) structured member feedback collection. These functions are currently performed by human coordinators operating from personal knowledge — an approach that is high-cost, fragile to staff turnover, and structurally excludes lower-capacity member organizations.

The research does not surface any existing tool that addresses the cross-org position mapping problem: existing advocacy software (Quorum, NationBuilder, CiviCRM) focuses on constituent management and legislative tracking, not on surfacing alignment/divergence across peer organizations in a coalition. The combination of tech heterogeneity and ideological breadth in most coalitions creates a structural challenge that no current software product addresses.

Finding 8 adds a dimension that is frequently overlooked in civic tech discussions: even if better position-mapping and coordination tools existed, they would fail without a parallel feedback infrastructure. Coalitions currently cannot distinguish between member silence that signals satisfaction and member silence that precedes exit. The coordinator is simultaneously the relationship broker and the feedback collector — a structural conflict of interest that suppresses honest input. Any system designed to support coalition coordination must account for this feedback gap: providing lightweight, low-friction, and structurally independent channels for members to signal what they actually think, not just what they're willing to say to the coordinator.

This represents a clear and underserved user need in the civic tech and advocacy space.

## User Journey Map

### User Type 1: Coalition Director / Field-Builder Staff

The primary user — the person responsible for managing the coalition day-to-day, brokering relationships between member orgs, and ensuring the coalition can speak with a coherent voice.

| Stage | What they're doing | What they're thinking | What they're feeling | Pain points |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Onboarding to coalition** | Reviewing membership lists, past meeting notes, existing shared drives; calling predecessor or longtime members | "Who are all these orgs? Where do they stand on the key issues? Who knows whom?" | Overwhelmed, reliant on oral history | No structured record of member org positions; institutional memory lives with departing staff |
| **Mapping the landscape** | Manually reading position papers, website policy pages, past public statements from each member org | "I need to understand where we agree and where we'll have conflict before I can do anything" | Intellectually stretched, time-pressured | No tooling to surface alignment/divergence across documents; synthesis is entirely manual |
| **Pre-meeting preparation** | Drafting agendas, writing bespoke briefing notes for each member org about who they'll meet and why | "I need each org to walk in knowing enough to have a productive conversation with orgs they've never met" | Stressed, doing high-effort custom work for each relationship | No standardized briefing format or tool; relationship context is in coordinator's head |
| **Facilitating cross-org connections** | Making warm introductions via email; setting up bilateral calls; attending meetings to hold context | "I'm the only one who knows why these two orgs should talk to each other" | Indispensable but bottlenecked | No shared relationship map; brokering is fully dependent on coordinator being present |
| **Managing position divergence** | Identifying where member orgs can't agree; negotiating compromise positions; knowing when to surface vs. suppress disagreement | "We can't go to policymakers with conflicting positions — I have to figure out our actual collective stance" | Anxious, diplomatically pressured | No system to formally track or visualize position agreements and divergences across member orgs |
| **Collecting member feedback** | Asking "any thoughts?" at the end of meetings; following up informally with a few trusted contacts; never sure whether silence means agreement or disengagement | "I hear from the same five orgs every time. I don't know what the other twelve actually think." | Uncertain, structurally blind to dissent | No mechanism for anonymous or structured feedback; coordinator is both relationship broker and feedback collector, suppressing honest input |
| **Managing tech-capacity gaps** | Simplifying communication for low-tech member orgs (phone calls instead of shared docs); doing extra data entry; re-sending materials in multiple formats | "Some of our most important community orgs are never going to use the platform — I have to meet them where they are" | Frustrated, doing redundant work | Tech fragmentation creates two-tier participation; coordinator absorbs the gap manually |
| **Cross-ideological navigation** | Building personal trust with orgs from different political traditions; finding shared-interest framing; managing messaging to avoid alienating conservative or progressive members | "I need language that everyone can sign on to without compromising their identity" | Walking a tightrope, cautious | No tooling to identify overlap zones; depends entirely on coordinator's political judgment and relationships |
| **Sustaining the coalition** | Recruiting new members; keeping existing members engaged; justifying value back to funders | "Members are getting fatigued. Funders want metrics. I'm doing the work of three people." | Burned out, under-resourced | Coordination is inherently people-intensive with no automation support; staff burnout is endemic |

### User Type 2: Member Organization Staff (Coalition Participant)

The staff person at a member organization who participates in coalition activities — attends meetings, reads coalition communications, contributes their org's perspective, and takes action items back.

| Stage | What they're doing | What they're thinking | What they're feeling | Pain points |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Joining the coalition** | Receiving onboarding materials; learning who the other member orgs are; attending first few meetings | "What does my org's membership in this coalition actually mean? What will I be expected to do?" | Uncertain, somewhat passive | Onboarding is unstructured; no clear picture of who the other orgs are or where they stand |
| **Pre-meeting preparation** | Skimming meeting agendas sent by coalition staff; reviewing their own org's position; little to no briefing on peer org positions | "I know what we think, but I don't know what the other orgs will say or what they need from us" | Underprepared, going in cold | No briefing on peer org positions or relationship context before cross-org meetings |
| **In cross-org meetings** | Representing their org's position; discovering disagreements in real time; sometimes surprised by where other orgs stand | "I didn't know they had published a position paper on this — now I'm caught flat-footed" | Reactive, sometimes defensive | Position divergences surface in meetings rather than being surfaced and resolved in advance |
| **Post-meeting follow-up** | Drafting action items; reporting back to their org's leadership; uncertain whether coalition commitments align with their org's public positions | "Did we just commit to something that contradicts our official position? I need to check." | Anxious, unsure of boundaries | No clear system for tracking what their org committed to vs. what they can actually endorse |
| **Giving feedback on coalition direction** | Receiving occasional surveys or being asked verbally at meetings; often doesn't complete surveys due to bandwidth; hesitant to voice dissent with the coordinator directly | "I filled out a survey six months ago and nothing changed. Why would I bother again? And I don't want to seem difficult." | Skeptical, self-censoring | Feedback loops don't close; power dynamics suppress candid input; no anonymous channel exists |
| **Low-tech member experience** | Missing online discussions; unable to use shared platforms; receiving information via phone and email forwarded by coalition staff | "I feel out of the loop. I never know what's been decided until after the fact." | Excluded, second-tier | Tech gaps create a participation tier where low-capacity orgs are systematically less informed and less influential |
| **Cross-ideological navigation** | Navigating conversations with orgs whose values differ significantly from their own | "I agree with them on this issue but not on others — how much should I trust them as partners?" | Cautious, politically aware | No framework or pre-meeting briefing to help understand the ideological context of partner orgs |
